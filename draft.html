Grace Hopper

(December 9, 1906 - January 1, 1992)

Timeline:

  • 1906: Born in New York City; oldest of three children
  • At age 7, she dismantled an alarm clock to see how it worked
  • 1928: BA in Mathematics and Physics from Vassar College
  • 1930: MA from Yale University
  • 1930: Married New York University professor Vincent Foster Hopper (1906-1976)
  • 1931: Began teaching mathematics at Vassar
  • 1934: Earned Ph.D under tutelage of Øystein Ore; had her dissertation, New Types of Irreducibility Criteria, published
  • 1943: Sworn into U.S. Navy Reserve during WWII; served in the WAVES (Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service)
  • 1944: Graduated first in her class at the Naval Reserve Midshipmen's School at Smith College in Northampton, Massachusetts; served on Mark I computer programming staff
  • 1949: Turned down a professorship at Vassar to work as a research fellow under a Navy contract at Harvard
  • 1949: Hired as a senior mathematician at Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation; was on the team developing the UNIVAC I
  • 1952: Developed the A compiler; first version: A-0
  • 1954: Named as the first director of automatic programming in her company; her department released some of the first compiler languages, among them MATH-MATIC and FLOW-MATIC
  • 1959: Served as a technical consultant to the CODASYL (Conference on Data Systems Languages); the committee defined the language COBOL (COmmon Business-Oriented Language)
  • 1967: Began serving as director of the Navy Programming Languages Group in the Navy's Office of Information Systems Planning
  • 1973: Promoted to Captain
  • 1983: Promoted to Commodore (later renamed Rear Admiral in 1985)
  • 1986: Involuntarily retires from Navy
  • 1992: Passes away in Arlington, VA

Facts:

  • Her great-grandfather was Admiral Alexander Wilson Russell who faught in the Battle of Mobile Bay during the Civil War
  • Despite divorcing her husband in 1945, she retained his last name
  • She was under the Naval minimum weight (120 lbs) by 15 lbs, and had to obtain exemption to enlist
  • Her request to transfer to the regular Navy after the war was declined because she was 38
  • People were skeptical of her compiler due to the fact that at that point, computers could only perform simple arithmetic
  • Her philosophy was that programming should be defined in a language like English and translated to machine code
  • Advocated for the replacement of large centralized systems with a network of smaller computers
  • Developed the standards for testing computer systems with many of the early programming languages, such as FORTRAN and COBOL; these tests became standard for the National Bureau of Standards (NBS), later the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)
  • Retired various times from the Navy, but came back to the duty many times as well; she officially retired at age 79
  • Was a senior consultant to Digital Equipment Corporation until her death; she served as a goodwill ambassador
  • She was the one that came up with the term "bug" in computer science, when a moth was stuck in a relay and disrupted its operation
